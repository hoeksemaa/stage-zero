REASONING AND EPISTEMIC CERTAINTY
The NIS is a 20% stratified sample of all US community hospital discharges, weighted by a factor of 5 to produce national estimates. For TIPS, this means each year's estimate is derived from roughly 1,000-1,100 actual observed discharges. The NIS covers more than 97% of the US population across all payers and is the standard dataset used in virtually all published US procedure volume research for TIPS.

These estimates are directionally reliable but not precise measurements. TIPS is concentrated at a relatively small number of high-volume academic centers, which makes the estimate more sensitive to which hospitals happen to fall in the sample in a given year. Unstated confidence intervals are probably +/-15-20%, meaning the true annual volume could plausibly range from roughly 4,500 to 6,500. The claim that US TIPS volume is approximately 5,000 per year is well-supported; the claim that any specific year differs meaningfully from another (e.g., a COVID dip in 2020) is less certain, as year-to-year differences of 200-400 procedures are within sampling noise. Consistency with multiple independent NIS-based studies covering 1998-2013, which all report ~5,500-6,000/yr, provides additional confidence that the order of magnitude is correct. No independent non-NIS data source was used to cross-validate. For TAM purposes, "approximately 5,000 US TIPS procedures per year" is a defensible estimate from the best available public data.
